Enterprise AI Program

One AI Platform for the Organization

Recommendation and business case: ChatGPT Enterprise + Codex on the GPT-5.6 model family — one vendor for every desk, a governed pilot before every dollar of scale.

AI Specialist, Technology & Innovation · July 2026 · DRAFT v1
Speaker notes

Executive summary

Recommend OpenAI across the board — piloted, measured, reversible

  • What: ChatGPT for knowledge workers (Business tier now, Enterprise at scale) + Codex for our ~15 heavy developers — one vendor, one privacy review, one training program, on the frontier GPT-5.6 models.
  • Why this vendor: highest likelihood of actual adoption (employees already prefer it), live Canadian data-at-rest residency, and a single credit pool covering both the office and coding lanes.
  • Why now: staff AI use is happening regardless — the documented public-sector failure (Ontario, 2026) is not sanctioning a vetted tool. Alberta shows what the governed version returns.
  • Risk posture: platform layer stays vendor-neutral so the coding lane can add alternatives without re-procurement; one residency gap (US inference) is a named, manageable PIA item.
The ask

Approve a 90-day pilot (~$40K USD year-one run rate), sponsor the Privacy Impact Assessment, and authorize the OpenAI sales conversation.

Everything on this slide is expanded later — if the room only reads one slide, it's this one. The ask is small and specific on purpose: a pilot decision, not a platform commitment.

3
Why now

Ungoverned AI use is the risk; a sanctioned tool is the mitigation

The cautionary tale — Ontario, 2026

  • Auditor General found ~12,000 staff using ~400 AI websites; 60% rated unsafe.
  • Only one sanctioned tool existed — so 94% of usage went to unvetted consumer sites, including sensitive data pasted into foreign services.
  • The failure wasn't adopting AI. It was failing to give staff a governed way to.

The proof next door — Alberta, 2025–26

  • Volunteer cohort → academy → governed platform: 2,000+ public servants trained, 1,000+ apps.
  • Delivery proof: a 5-month application rebuilt in 4 days; an 11-week build for $108K vs $1.3–1.9M estimated.
  • Their playbook is open-source — we don't start from zero.

No Saskatchewan Crown has a documented program yet — we would set the provincial standard rather than follow it.

Speaker notes

Sources: Ontario AG 2026 AI audit (auditor.on.ca); Alberta Velocity Whitepapers + Anthropic case study. Alberta figures are first-party — frame as "Alberta's reported results, to validate in our own pilot." The Saskatchewan white-space point lands well at director level: leadership story, de-risked by the precedent next door.

Recommendation

One vendor at the seat layer; vendor-neutral underneath

  • Knowledge workers: ChatGPT Business ($20/seat/mo, self-serve) for the pilot; graduate to Enterprise (residency, SCIM, compliance API) as seats scale.
  • Developers (~15): Codex — bundled with seats, budgeted separately for heavy usage (~$100–200/dev/mo in credits), spend caps from day one.
  • Models: GPT-5.6 family — Sol for hard reasoning, Terra as default, Luna for high-volume cheap tasks.
  • The architecture condition: our platform layer (document intelligence, agent harness, automation library) is built MCP-based and gateway-routed — no vendor lock-in in our own code. Seats are a contract; architecture is the commitment.
Why this shape

Every strong Canadian adopter (Shopify, Wealthsimple, CIBC) runs tools behind one governed gateway. If Codex disappoints our 15 devs, we add an alternative for them without re-procurement or migration.

What we're NOT doing

No tenant-wide license wave. No second or third seat platform. No production use before the PIA closes. Expansion happens on pilot evidence, not projections.

Speaker notes

The reversibility condition is what makes a single-vendor recommendation defensible — it converts "betting on OpenAI" into "starting with OpenAI." If challenged on why not Microsoft (incumbent): adoption data; why not Anthropic (better coder): feasibility and single-vendor simplicity, and the architecture keeps that door open.

8
Privacy & compliance

The path is defined; one gap is named and manageable

  • Legal position: Saskatchewan FOIP has no data-localization requirement — residency is regulator preference, not law. The binding gates are a Privacy Impact Assessment and a written agreement with safeguards (SK IPC guidance).
  • What OpenAI provides: no training on business data (default), SOC 2 Type II / ISO 27001, SSO/SCIM, compliance API, 30-day configurable retention, Canadian data-at-rest residency at no extra cost.
  • The gap: model inference runs in the US today. Handled the standard way: written vendor confirmation of scope → PIA risk assessment → documented risk acceptance for transient cross-border processing. Alberta accepted the equivalent risk shape.
  • Hard rules regardless of vendor: no personal information in prompts during pilot; staff training before access; usage audit via the compliance API.
Pre-signature item #1

OpenAI's written answer on inference location and zero-retention scope for the ChatGPT product (confirmed today only for the API path). This single letter determines the PIA's central paragraph.

Speaker notes

Full legal detail: reference/canadian-data-requirements.md (FOIP analysis, IPC guidance links, vendor scorecard). If privacy raises "why not the vendor with in-country processing": Microsoft's Canada in-country processing has no GA date either — every lane requires the same risk-acceptance shape today, so it doesn't differentiate.
